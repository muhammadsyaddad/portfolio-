# The Big Picture

When learning complex subjects that take a long time to master, it helps to
see the big picture early. That map becomes useful later, especially when the
details start to feel dense, because it shows where you currently stand in the
field and how each part connects to the rest.

Below is a simple breakdown of three subjects: Physics, Mathematics, and
Software Engineering.

## Physics

Physics can be viewed through seven major topics. Some of them function as
leverage topics, meaning they shape how easily you can understand the others.

1. Classical Mechanics
2. Quantum Mechanics
3. Thermodynamics
4. Electromagnetism
5. Relativity
6. Cosmology
7. Optics

Classical Mechanics and Quantum Mechanics are especially important because they
provide foundational intuition that carries into many later topics.

## Mathematics

Mathematics can be broken into a few core pillars, each answering a different
kind of question:

1. Number Theory: How many?
2. Algebra: How are they related?
3. Geometry: Where is the position?
4. Calculus: How fast is it changing?
5. Statistics: What is the likelihood?

This framing helps reduce the feeling that mathematics is just a collection of
unrelated techniques. Each pillar studies a different aspect of structure,
space, change, or uncertainty.

## Software Engineering

Software Engineering also has its own core pillars:

1. Programming Languages
2. Data Structures and Algorithms
3. Front-end Development
4. Back-end Development
5. Cloud Services

Even though the field feels broad, the big picture is still manageable when
viewed at this level.

## Summary

At the big-picture level, none of this looks overwhelmingly large. That is part
of the benefit. Having a map is helpful when you are halfway through a long
journey because it reminds you how much you have already learned and what still
remains ahead.

Some pillars have more influence than others. In Physics, for example, once you
understand Classical Mechanics and Quantum Mechanics, you arguably hold around
40 to 50 percent of the overall picture, because those subjects provide much of
the leverage needed to understand the rest.
